Figurative Language

Foreshadowing

Definition

Foreshadowing is a type of figurative language that authors use to build suspense and tension for what will happen next. It is also a tool writers use to give hints at what will come later in their story/what events are too come.

Example From Esperenza Rising

"When we arrived, nothing was as promised. There were many hard times. But life was also exciting" (Pg. 49) This is foreshadowing because it shows how Abuela has immigrated before and it seems to relate her experiences to Esperanza’s possible experiences.

Real-world Example

Sometimes if someone/you are being negative, you might foreshadow what will happen/what will happen to you. Also, people could foreshadow that stocks could crash and the next day they do. Foreshadowing is everywhere in real life!

Imagery

Definition

Imagery is a type of figurative language that uses extreme detail, the five senses, and evokes mental images of different scenes, (see examples).

Example From Esperenza Rising

“The field was still surrounded by the chain-link fence, but no one was protecting the entrance 211 this time. All the evidence of people she had seen before was there, but not one person was to be seen. Laundry waved on the clothesline. Plates with rice and beans sat on crates and swarmed with busy flies. Shoes were lined up in front of tents, as if waiting for someone to step into them. The breeze picked up loose newspapers and floated them across the field. It was quiet and desolate, except for the goat still tied to the tree, bleating for freedom.” (Pg. 211-212)

This is imagery because it describes a scene completely, with extreme detail, that evokes a mental image and uses senses.

Real-world Example

Imagery is used all the time in real life! Whether you’re describing what happened in your day, or your favorite food, you’re using imagery! Any time you describe something in detail or describe something using one/multiple senses, its imagery.

Metaphor

Definition

A metaphor is a type of figurative language based on making connections between multiple/two things/ideas. Metaphors don’t use comparison words, (like, as, etc.) they just state things implicitly. Metaphors are like similes, but similes use comparison words while metaphors don’t.

Example From Esperenza Rising

“After you have lived many mountains and valleys, we will be together.” (Pg. 51)

This is a metaphor because it relates life to “mountains and valleys,” implicitly and without comparison words.

Real-world Example

Do you ever make connections to things by just stating them instead of adding comparison words? For example, saying, “School is a prison,” or “They have a heart of gold,”. That’s a metaphor! You probably say around 6 metaphors a minute, depending on how quick you talk, and you probably don’t even notice it! Metaphors are so embedded in our language that we practically can't live without them.

Personification

Definition

Personification is a form of figurative language that occurs when human characteristics are applied to an object or something nonhuman, (Refer to the examples). It is like anthropomorphism, but anthropomorphism is only human characteristics applied to animals.

Example From Esperenza Rising

“This whole valley breathes and lives,” he said, sweeping his arm toward the distant mountains that guarded them. “It gives us the grapes and then they welcome us.” He gently touched a wild tendril that reached into the row, as if it had been waiting to shake his hand. He picked up a handful of earth and studied it. “Did you know that when you lie down on the land, you can feel it breathe? That you can feel its heart beating?” (Pg. 1)

This is personification because it states how the "valley breathes and lives," giving the valley human traits and "they welcome us" this also gives inatimate objects human qualities.

Real-world Example

Have you thought/felt as though your alarm clock was yelling at you when it goes off? That’s personification! Have you ever heard someone say something about food calling their name? That’s also personification! There are many ways personification is in your life, even right now!

Simile

Definition

A simile is a type of figurative language that directly compares two things by using like or as, (see examples). Similes are different from metaphors because of those comparison words, (like, as, etc.), metaphors only use implicit comparisons, (no comparison words).

Example From Esperenza Rising

“Then, like a caterpillar, she slowly inched flat next to him, their faces looking at each other.” (Pg. 2)

This is a simile because it compares her to a catipillar using the word "like".

Real-world Example

Have you ever compared something by saying like or as? For example, “Did you see her? She ran like the wind!”. That’s a simile! You might be using similes in your everyday life without even noticing! Some more examples are, “She was as sly as a fox.” and “Watching that show was like watching paint dry.”.

Symbolism

Definition

Symbolism is a type of figurative language that uses a symbol, object, or word to represent an abstract idea, (see examples). Symbolism hints at its true meaning instead of blatantly saying it’s meaning. Symbolism can also be used to suggest moods and emotions.

Example From Esperenza Rising

“Abuelita smiled, reached over, and pulled the yarn, unraveling all of Esperanza’s rows. “Do not be afraid to start over,” she said.” (Pg. 15)

This is symbolism because it symbolizes a deeper meaning/a life lesson, (Don’t be afraid to start over in life,).

Real-world Example

People hint at people all the time using symbolism in real life! For example, white stands for life and purity and black usually stands for death.
